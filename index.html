Project Overview

New Yorkers have faced sweltering heat reaching feels-like temperatures upwards of 130ºF on subway platforms which have caused health issues, discomfort, and decreased ridership numbers. As of 2024, the MTA has implemented air conditioning units and fans in select stations, however these are high-cost, produce harmful carbon emissions, and their installation causes delays in transportation. These concerns inspired the design of the Thermo-Fluids Module (TFM). Prioritizing safety, installation ease, and affordability has been the focus of the scope for this project as the goal is to install the TFM across all NYC subway platforms. Initial design iterations implemented a fully passive system, with the goal of maintaining a 5º temperature decrease on the platform, as this provides a noticeable difference in temperature for riders. Based on initial hand calculations, this cannot be supported sustainably or efficiently without some active assistance. The current design takes inspiration from a thermosiphon, a gravity-driven, fully passive heat exchanger, and a heat sink. Heat sinks are typically only seen on the microchip-size scale, but similar principles of fin efficiency and forced convection are present in our system. The thermosiphon is composed of a lower heat intake region and an upper heat output region. Fluid in the intake region is heated, expands, and travels upwards into the output tank. As it rises, it displaces the cooler fluid downward into the intake region, enforcing a natural convection cycle. Mapped over the subway station, the heat intake region is located at the subway platform, and is fulfilled by the TFM. For transferring the heat energy to the heat output region, water– selected for its high heat capacity and nontoxic, inexpensive, and renewable properties– runs through flexible tubing into an uninsulated aluminum tank positioned approximately 25 ft above the TFM, just beneath the sidewalk grates. An off-the-shelf fountain pump was incorporated as the active element on the output valve of the TFM to increase the mass flow rate of the water and overall heat transfer rate through the cooling system. A key design feature is the fins on the TFM, angled 30º into the airflow generated by trains entering and exiting the station, functioning as a heat sink by increasing surface area and improving convective heat transfer. The TFM is inherently adaptable to a greater subway network, allowing for multiple modules to be installed based on station sizes and needs. The versatility of the design further emphasizes its potential impact on cooling New York City. With temperatures rising every summer, the TFM cooling system presents a cost-effective, eco-friendly, scalable solution that lessens hazardous health risks and eases the discomfort of riders waiting on the subway platform.

Our Design

The TFM body is constructed as a six-sided enclosure measuring 24 in × 12 in × 12 in, fabricated from 0.25-in-thick 6061 aluminum sheet metal. The panels are joined using zinc-plated corner brackets and L-shaped supports to provide structural rigidity while maintaining a watertight assembly. Aluminum 6061 was selected due to its high thermal conductivity, corrosion resistance, and ease of fabrication, making it well suited for heat transfer applications in humid subway environments. The front face of the module incorporates 24 narrow slots that are extruded-cut at a 30° angle relative to the surface. These angled openings house 24 aluminum fins, each measuring 5.5 in in length, 3.5 in in width, and 0.032 in thick. The fins extend approximately 1.95 in outside the module, with the remaining portion submerged internally and in direct contact with the working fluid, water. This configuration maximizes heat transfer by allowing thermal energy to conduct from the internal fluid through the fins and dissipate to the surrounding air. Each fin includes two holes, one at the top and one at the bottom, through which 26-in-long rods with a 0.13-in diameter are inserted. These rods maintain uniform spacing between fins, ensuring consistent airflow and improving convective performance. Additionally, mounting the fins on rods simplifies both manufacturing and maintenance, as the entire fin assembly can be installed or removed as a single unit. The rods are secured to the module using thin flat brackets, further streamlining assembly. Internally, the module is filled with water, which serves as the working fluid to promote convection across the submerged fin surfaces. A 500 GPH fountain pump is positioned at the bottom of the enclosure to enhance fluid circulation and improve heat transfer within the module. By actively moving the water, the pump helps prevent thermal stratification and ensures uniform temperature distribution. The pump is connected to PVC piping that routes fluid upward and out of the TFM, forming the connection to the HRU. The HRU is constructed from six sheets of the same 6061 aluminum material to maintain thermal compatibility with the TFM. The unit includes two flanged ports located on the left and right faces, each containing mounting holes for secure attachment of the PVC tubing. One flange connects to the outlet from the pump, while the other returns fluid to the TFM. This configuration enables continuous fluid circulation between the TFM and HRU, supporting the thermosyphon-inspired operation in which heated fluid is transported to the HRU, cooled, and returned to the module. Through this combined convection-driven and assisted circulation process, thermal energy is effectively transferred from the working fluid to the surrounding environment.
