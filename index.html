Project Overview

New Yorkers have faced sweltering heat reaching feels-like temperatures upwards of 130ºF on subway platforms which have caused health issues, discomfort, and decreased ridership numbers. As of 2024, the MTA has implemented air conditioning units and fans in select stations, however these are high-cost, produce harmful carbon emissions, and their installation causes delays in transportation.These concerns inspired the design of the Thermo-Fluids Module (TFM). Prioritizing safety, installation ease, and affordability has been the focus of the scope for this project as the goal is to install the TFM across all NYC subway platforms.

Initial design iterations implemented a fully passive system, with the goal of maintaining a 5º temperature decrease on the platform, as this provides a noticeable difference in temperature for riders. Based on initial hand calculations, this cannot be supported sustainably or efficiently without some active assistance. The current design takes inspiration from a thermosyphon, a gravity-driven, fully passive heat exchanger, and a heat sink. Heat sinks are typically only seen on the microchip-size scale, but similar principles of fin efficiency and forced convection are present in our system.The thermosyphon is composed of a lower heat intake region and an upper heat output region. Fluid in the intake region is heated, expands, and travels upwards into the output tank. As it rises, it displaces the cooler fluid downward into the intake region, enforcing a natural convection cycle. Mapped over the subway station, the heat intake region is located at the subway platform, and is fulfilled by the TFM.

For transferring the heat energy to the heat output region, water, selected for its high heat capacity and nontoxic, inexpensive, and renewable properties, runs through flexible tubing into an uninsulated aluminum tank positioned approximately 25 ft above the TFM, just beneath the sidewalk grates. An off-the-shelf fountain pump was incorporated as the active element on the output valve of the TFM to increase the mass flow rate of the water and overall heat transfer rate through the cooling system.

A key design feature is the fins on the TFM, angled 30º into the airflow generated by trains entering and exiting the station, functioning as a heat sink by increasing surface area and improving convective heat transfer. These fits sit in the front face partially outside of the module and partially inside -- making contact with the internal fluid, water, and the induced airflow caused by train arrival and departure.

The TFM is inherently adaptable to a greater subway network, allowing for multiple modules to be installed based on station sizes and needs. The versatility of the design further emphasizes its potential impact on cooling New York City. With temperatures rising every summer, the TFM cooling system presents a cost-effective, eco-friendly, scalable solution that lessens hazardous health risks and eases the discomfort of riders waiting on the subway platform.

Our Full-Scale Design

The TFM body is constructed as a six-sided enclosure measuring 24 in × 12 in × 12 in, fabricated from 0.25-in-thick 6061 aluminum sheet metal. The panels are joined using zinc-plated corner brackets and L-shaped supports to provide structural rigidity while maintaining a watertight assembly. Aluminum 6061 was selected due to its high thermal conductivity, corrosion resistance, and ease of fabrication, making it well suited for heat transfer applications in humid subway environments.

The front face of the module incorporates 24 narrow slots that are extruded-cut at a 30° angle relative to the surface. These angled openings house 24 aluminum fins, each measuring 11.0 inches in length, 7.0 inches in width, and 0.032 inches thick. The fins extend approximately 3.90 in outside the module, with the remaining portion submerged internally and in direct contact with the working fluid, water.

This configuration maximizes heat transfer by allowing thermal energy to conduct from the internal fluid through the fins and dissipate to the surrounding air. Each fin includes two holes, one at the top and one at the bottom, through which 26-in-long aluminium rods with a 0.13-in diameter are inserted. These rods maintain uniform spacing between fins, ensuring consistent airflow and improving convective performance. Additionally, mounting the fins on rods simplifies both manufacturing and maintenance, as the entire fin assembly can be installed or removed as a single unit. The rods are secured to the module using thin flat brackets, further streamlining assembly.

Internally, the module is filled with water, which serves as the working fluid to promote convection across the submerged fin surfaces. A 500 GPH fountain pump is positioned at the bottom of the enclosure to enhance fluid circulation and improve heat transfer within the module. By actively moving the water, the pump helps prevent thermal stratification and ensures uniform temperature distribution. The pump is connected to PVC piping that routes fluid upward and out of the TFM, forming the connection to the HRU.

The HRU is constructed from six sheets of the same 6061 aluminum material to maintain thermal compatibility with the TFM. The unit includes two flanged ports located on the left and right faces, each containing mounting holes for secure attachment of the PVC tubing. One flange connects to the outlet from the pump, while the other returns fluid to the TFM. This configuration enables continuous fluid circulation between the TFM and HRU, supporting the thermosyphon-inspired operation in which heated fluid is transported to the HRU, cooled, and returned to the module. Through this combined convection-driven and assisted circulation process, thermal energy is effectively transferred from the working fluid to the surrounding environment.

Our Half-Scale Prototype Design

The TFM body is constructed as a six-sided enclosure measuring 12 in × 6 in × 6 in, fabricated from 0.25-in-thick 6061 aluminum sheet metal. The panels are joined using zinc-plated corner brackets and L-shaped supports to provide structural rigidity while maintaining a watertight assembly. In order to meet design geometry, corner-brakets and front-face brackets were handmade out of recycled aluminium sheet metal.

The front face of the module incorporates 24 narrow slots that are extruded-cut at a 30° angle relative to the surface. These angled openings house 24 aluminum fins, each measuring 5.5 in long, 3.5 inch width, and 0.032 in thick. The fins extend approximately 1.95 in outside the module, with the remaining portion submerged internally and in direct contact with the working fluid, water. This configuration maximizes heat transfer by allowing thermal energy to conduct from the internal fluid through the fins and dissipate to the surrounding air. Each fin includes two holes, one at the top and one at the bottom, through which 13-in-long rods with a 0.13-in diameter are inserted. These rods maintain uniform spacing between fins, ensuring consistent airflow and improving convective performance. The rods are secured to the module using thin flat brackets.

Internally, the module is filled with water, which serves as the working fluid to promote convection across the submerged fin surfaces. A 140 GPH fountain pump is positioned at the bottom of the enclosure to enhance fluid circulation and improve heat transfer within the module. By actively moving the water, the pump helps prevent thermal stratification and ensures uniform temperature distribution. The pump is connected to PVC piping that routes fluid upward and out of the TFM, forming the connection to the HRU.

Due to cost-constraints and material shortges, the Heat Release Unit (HRU) is being simulated using a 1 gallon bucket. This bucket has the pipe inlet, connected to the pump, and the pipe outlet, connecting the system back to the TFM. Although the results will not match the full-scale alumnium box, the bucket supports the system's thermosyphon inspired cycle and confirms pump functionality.

Simulation Results

Transient Analysis

A transient thermal simulation was conducted on the TFM to observe its performance during a train pass, defined as the train entering the station, stopping for passengers, and departing, on a 90°F summer day. A train speed of 17.4 mph at an air temperature of 100°F was used to calculate the convective coefficient of 7.39E-8 (BTU/s·ft²·°F).

The average heat flux and temperature of the module converged in under 10 seconds, with the interior portion of the fins taking the longest to reach steady-state. This indicates that the system reaches functional capacity while the train is entering the station, and the time the train spends in the station stopped for passengers and then exiting does not significantly disturb the flowfield. This short settling time indicates that the system is highly reliable and predictable, and the terminal values were determined to be valid for use in subsequent calculations.

Using the flux results, the heat load calculated to be entering the station was 0.98 BTU/s. This value was used to determine the pump requirements and temperature-reduction capabilities of the TFM. Based on temperature results at the module inlet and outlet, the water temperature is expected to increase by 4°F as it passes through the TFM. Using this value, it was determined the pump must deliver a flow rate of 106 gallons per hour. Using the sensible heat equation, it was calculated that a single full-scale TFM can reduce the temperature within the platform area by 0.58 ºF. Since a temperature difference of 1°F is considered to be the threshold for a noticeable difference to the human body, 2 TFMs would be needed to produce a noticeable effect. However, to achieve the target temperature reduction of 5 ºF, 9 TFMs would be required in this station. For detailed analysis, please refer to the full Design Report.

Steady-State Analysis

A steady-state computational fluid dynamics (CFD) simulation was performed using ANSYS Fluent to evaluate the thermal performance of the fin assembly under continuous operating conditions. Due to meshing constraints, a simplified fin-rod assembly representing the scaled prototype were used. An air enclosure was created around the 24 aluminum fins to simulate the subway environment.

In order to run this simulation the following boundary conditions were applied: a velocity inlet of 25.52 ft/sec at 98.33 ºF, a pressure outlet with a 0 Pa guage pressure and a backflow temperature of 98.33 ºF, and a symmetry condition appled to remaining enclosure walls added to simulate the station enviroment. Material properties for 6061 aluminum and air were defined according to standard values.

The simulation results yielded an area-weighted average convective heat transfer coefficient of 45.08 BTU/(hr·ft²·°F). and a total heat transfer rate of 2456.74 BTU/hr. This value of the heat transfer coefficient is significantly higher than both the analytical and transient results. This discrepancy suggests that the steady-state simulation overestimates convective performance, likely due to mesh resolution limitations at the fluid–solid interface. Additionally, using the total surface heat flux of 36.37 BTU/(h·ft²) from the simulation, and the total area of the fins of 3.98 ft², the total heat dissipation is estimated to be 259.32 BTU/hr per module. This results in a very low temperature difference, and does not meet the desired 5°F temperature reduction. This simulation is testing the passive cooling capacity of the fins alone, whereas the final prototype design incorporates a 140 GHP fountain pump. The addition of this pump significantly enhances the convective heat transfer within the system. Using water thermal properties, the total heat removal capacity increases to approximately 11,532.74 BTU/hr which is 44 times greater than the fins passive cooling capacity. Based on these results, the single, scaled prototype provides only localized cooling, however the results demonstrate that following our initial goal of having multiple modules wsithin the station, can produce noticeable temperature reductions.

Physical Testing

The physical testing plan includes vibration of model, operation test of pump and system, weather resilliance, and a circulation test. The purpose of each of these is to ensure that the TFM will operate properly within its intended enviroment.

Pump Validation

In an effort to validate the pump performace, a circulation test was conducted. This test successfully demonstrated that the experimental setup could effectively imitate a closed-loop circulation system while providing reliable performance data. The system operated without major functional issues, confirming that the pump, tubing, and connections were properly assembled and capable of continuous operation.

Using the change in water height method, the volumetric and mass flow rates were calculated for two pump settings, resulting in values of approximately 91.63 GPH (0.0964 kg/s) for the highest setting and 90.62 GPH (0.0953 kg/s) for the second lowest setting . The similarity in flow rates across both settings suggests that the pump operates near a consistent output under the given conditions, likely influenced by system resistance and pumping height. Overall, this testing validated the pump setup, confirmed its ability to maintain circulation, and provided a realistic estimate of flow behavior within the intended system, supporting its use in the final design.
